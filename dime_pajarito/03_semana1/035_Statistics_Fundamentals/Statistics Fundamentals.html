Statistics Fundamentals¶

Statistics is usually defined as the science of drawing conclusions in the presence of uncertainty. It is most useful in the collection, analysis, and interpretation of data. This discipline provides us with the fundamental principles that allow us to extract and understand that information. It also provides us with the necessary tools to verify the quality of our data.

Statistics has two branches:

  • Descriptive Statistics: Calulating the statistical parameters that describe the studied dataset. Some of the tools it uses are graphs, frequency measurements, centralization measurements, position measurements, dispersion measurements, among others. If these terms are unfamiliar to you, don't worry - we'll cover them soon.

  • Inferential Statistics: Studying how to draw general conclusions for the entire population from the study of a sample, and the degree of reliability and significance of the results obtained. Its main tools are sampling, parameter estimation, and hypothesis testing.

Basics of descriptive statistics¶

In descriptive statistics, different measures are used to try to describe the properties of our data. Some of the basic concepts are:

Mean¶

The mean gives the average value of the data points in a sample:

$$\bar{x} = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^N x_i$$

Standard deviation¶

The standard deviation gives the amount of variation or dispersion of the data points in a sample:

$$s = \sqrt{\frac{1}{N-1} \sum_{i=1}^N (x_i - \overline{x})^2}$$
In [125]:
def stddev(t):
    n = len(t)
    c = mean(t)
    ss = sum((x-c)**2 for x in t)
    pvar = ss/(n)
    return pvar**0.5

Correlation¶

Correlation tries to quantify the relationship that exists between two variables, in order to determine if changes in one of the variables seems to be related to changes in the other. If this is the case, we will say that the variables are correlated or that there exists a correlation between them. The correlation is positive when the values of the variables increase together, and it is negative when the value of one variable decreases while the value of the other variable increases.

Histograms and Distributions¶

A histogram displays the frequency of data occurrences in a bar graph. More specifically, it indicates how often each distinct value occurs in the dataset. The horizontal axis represents the values of the dataset and the vertical axis represents the number of times those values occur.

A histogram is a specific example of a distribution.

Continuous distributions allow data to take on an infinite number of possible values. Examples include:

  • Normal
  • Gamma
  • Chi-squared
  • Student's t
  • Pareto
  • etc.

Don't worry about what these distributions are yet - we will cover them later.

On the other hand, discrete distributions are those in which the data can only be one of a finite set of values. Histograms are discrete distributions because the possible values must come directly from the dataset from which they is created, which by definition must be a finite set. Other examples of discrete distributions include:

  • Poisson
  • Binomial
  • Hypergeometric
  • Bernoulli
  • etc.

Again, don't worry about what these distributions are - we will cover them later.

Symmetric and asymmetric distributions¶

As discussed, a normal distribution is symmetric. A distribution is symmetric when the mode, median, and mean are roughly equal. In more colloquial terms, the left and right halves of the distribution look identical, and both halves start with high "peaks" and taper off as one moves to the "tails".

A distribution is right-skewed - Positive Skew if the "tail" to the right of the mean is longer than the one to the left. In the same way, a distribution is left-skewed - Negative Skew if the "tail" to the left of the mean is longer than the one to the right. These are examples of asymmetric distributions.

Asymmetric distributions are often problematic, since most statistical methods are usually developed for normal distributions, which are symmetric. To overcome these problems, transformations are usually carried out on the data so that the resulting data more closely resembles a normal distribution. This will be discussed in more depth later.

Other measure is kurtosis:

Excess kurtosis is usually defined as kurt 3, measure of the "peakedness" of the probability distribution of a real-valued random variable.

  • Excess kurt for the normal distribution is 0 (i.e. 3 -3 = 0).
  • Negative excess equals lighter tails than a normal distribution.
  • Positive excess equals heavier tails than the normal.

Quartiles and boxplots¶

The quartiles are the three values of the statistical variable that divide a set of ordered data into four equal parts. Q1, Q2 and Q3 determine the values corresponding to 25%, 50% and 75% of the data. Q2 coincides with the median.

Boxplots present several important characteristics of a dataset, such as dispersion and symmetry, at the same time. Each box is created using the three quartiles, as well as the minimum and maximum values of the dataset. These graphs provide us with a wealth of information and are extremely useful for finding outliers and comparing datasets:

Basics of inferential statistics¶

Linear regression¶

Linear regression is one of the main tools of inferential statistics. The goal of regression analysis is to describe the relationship between one variable, called the dependent variable, and another set of variables, called independent variables. More specifically, regression analysis helps to understand how the typical value of the dependent variable changes when any of the independent variables is changed, while the other independent variables remain fixed. The end product is an estimation of the dependent variable as a function of the independent variables. Based on this regression function, we can attempt to make estimates about future events.

Linear regression is one of the simplest and most widely used techniques in regression analysis; however, it makes very rigid assumptions about the relationship between the dependent variable $y$ and the independent variable $x$; namely:

$$y = \beta_0 + \beta_1 * x$$

One of the most popular methods to perform linear regression is ordinary least squares (OLS).

Hypothesis testing¶

Hypothesis testing is an essential procedure in statistics. A hypothesis test evaluates two mutually exclusive statements about a population to determine which statement is best supported by the sample data. When we say that a finding is statistically significant, its thanks to a hypothesis test.

The first hypothesis is generally called the null hypothesis, and is a general statement or default view that there is no relationship between two measured phenomena, or no association among groups. It can be thought of as a status-quo assumption. For example, "Company X's production equals 50 units/per day".

The alternative hypothesis is the second hypothesis, and is set at odds to the null hypothesis. It is usually taken to mean that the observed phenomena in the data are the result of a real, non-random effect. For example, "Company X's production is not equal to 50 units/per day".

In order to evaluate between these two hypotheses, we need to indicate a threshold level of significance. This is the probability that the observed results are actually due to chance, assuming the null hypothesis were true. Since we cannot ever 100% prove that the null hypothesis is false, we must select a level of significance that is small but nonzero. Generally, 5% is used (although in theory you can set any level you like). So if the computed probability that the observed results are due to chance is less than 5%, we reject the null hypothesis and propose the alternative hypothesis; otherwise, we do not.

There are two types of errors we can make in doing this:

  1. Type I Error: Rejecting the null hypothesis when we should not. The probability of a Type I error is denoted by $\alpha$.
  2. Type II Error: Failing to reject the null hypothesis when we should. The probability of a Type II error is denoted by $\beta$.

Finally, we come to the $p$-value, which is our calculated probability of observing the results that we do assuming the null hypothesis is true. As discussed earlier, if this is under the level of significance (usually 5%), then you can claim to have reasonable evidence to reject the null hypothesis. It does NOT automatically imply a "meaningful" or "important" finding, however - that is for you to decide when considering the real-world relevance of your result.

$t - test$¶

A t-test is used to determine if there is a significant difference between the means of two groups. It is mostly used when the data would likely follow a normal distribution, like the set of outcomes of flipping a coin 100 times.

ttest_1samp:

Calculate the T-test for the mean of ONE group of scores.

This is a two-sided test for the null hypothesis that the expected value (mean) of a sample of independent observations a is equal to the given population mean, popmean.

ttest_ind:

Calculate the T-test for the means of two independent samples of scores.

This is a two-sided test for the null hypothesis that 2 independent samples have identical average (expected) values. This test assumes that the populations have identical variances by default.

Example : you have 10 ages and you are checking whether avg age is 30 or not.

The One Sample t Test determines whether the sample mean is statistically different from a known or hypothesised population mean. The One Sample t Test is a parametric test

Chi-square test¶

We use the chi-square test when we have two categorical variables from a single population. It is used to determine whether there is a significant association between the two variables.

For example, in an election survey, voters might be classified by gender (male or female) and voting preference (Democrat, Republican, or Independent). We could use a chi-square test for independence to determine whether gender is related to voting preference:
